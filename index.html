TED X GALILEO

Late this year, I had a really important realization that affected my life greatly. I took control of my life in a way I had never had before, doing what I wanted to be done. From the time I was about eight, I have been playing piano. Until about seventh grade, that was my only hobby. However, in seventh grade, I started computer programming and getting really into it. In eighth grade, I stopped programming and started skateboarding, because it was cool, and programming was too "nerdy". In ninth grade, I started making electronic music, forgetting skating, programming, and piano, because they all weren't "cool". This continued until the beginning of tenth grade when I started having my realization.

Tenth grade was a lot harder than ninth grade. I was taking an honors math class and a much harder Spanish class. I was also thinking a lot about college. I started a computer programming club with my friend, partly because I hoped it would look good on a college application. All of this academic pressure forced me to solely focus on school, completely ignoring my past hobbies. I thought they weren’t necessary, I thought I didn't have time for hobbies. This continued until late in the spring semester, when I had my big realization after wasting a ton of time one afternoon. I realized that without my hobbies, I was not happy, without my hobbies, I would do worse in school, I would get distracted more easily, I would waste time. Without my hobbies, I was a different person.So I decided to put effort into all of my hobbies. I would play piano everyday, I would code everyday, I would skate everyday, and I would make music everyday. At first it was hard. I would spend too much time on something and then not be able to do my homework, or vice versa. It took a couple of weeks to balance my priorities, but eventually, it worked. Now, I code everyday, I make music everyday, I skate everyday, and I play piano everyday.

To be able to take control of your life, and pursue something besides work or school, is an extremely important skill. I define this skill as agency, and without Galileo, I would never have learned about agency. Agency is ownership of your learning. It is being able to motivate yourself, do well, ask questions, turn in work on time, collaborate, and lead. If you have ownership of your learning, it allows you to take ownership of your life when it matters most. Galileo has taught me a lot in the two years that I have been in it. I have created my best academic work, and learned many important skills that I will be able to use for the rest of my life. I appreciate Galileo because not only did it teach me the regular curriculum, but it taught me about group learning, collaboration, leadership, and much more. After I understood agency, I became more focused and motivated, and was able to change the way I work both in and out of school. Next year will be very challenging but I will continue pursuing my hobbies and use the skills that Galileo has taught me to create a successful year.

Analyzing Rhetoric

David Remnick. "WHEN THE TOWERS FELL" The New Yorker September 12, 2011

In WHEN THE TOWERS FELL by David Remnick, the author uses comparisons to further his rhetoric and emphasize the significance of the 9/11 attacks and how they will effect the next decade plus. He assumes the reader has knowledge of 9/11, but that the reader does not have knoledge of the General Solcum disaster which he describes with vivid detail using quotes from observers.

Remnick addresses the average American citizen by making his points clear and literal. The article itself is about something that has shaped many aspects of American culture today and continues to affect Americans every day lives in one way or another. Remnick points out how indirectly related this things can be saying, "A decade later, we also continue to reckon not only with the violence that bin Laden inflicted but with the follies, the misjudgments, and the violence that, directly or indirectly, he provoked—the acts of government deception, illegal domestic surveillance, 'extraordinary rendition,' 'enhanced interrogation' waterboarding."

In the beginning of the article he makes an important comparison between two major losses that occurred in New York. The first loss was from the early 20th century when a group of German immigrants were killed on a steam boat heading into New York. This was no terrorist attack but more than 1000 people died. Similar to 911, the whole city saw what happened and the boat fire had a huge effect on the city and the nation. He then describes how directly after the attacks happened there seemed like there would be no clear end to the next era of post 9/11 America. However, "This past spring," Remnick says "history-in the shape of a Navy SEAL team-seemed to provide the era with some closing punctuation." which is true, the assassination of Osama Bin-Laden and the Arab Spring gave some punctuation to the era that was defined in 2001. Remnick reminds that reader that the tenth anniversary of 9/11 "is a marker, not an end.". Remnick’s purpose was hard to relate to. By saying that a new era was defined and that it was not ended by the killing of the man responsible for the attacks Remnick makes an argument made by many other people and has understood by the American public before.
